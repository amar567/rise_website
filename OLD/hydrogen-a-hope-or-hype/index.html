Hydrogen: A hope or hype?
-By Dr. S. Sivaram

The need for transition to low-carbon energy has necessitated exploring alternatives to fossil fuels. Hydrogen has emerged as a promising option and has become a centre piece of our conversation. There is a rush of literature, opinions, editorials, and conferences, some hailing it as our only last hope for a sustainable civilization and some calling it a mirage, a rainbow on the horizon, an unnecessary distraction when lower cost solutions are available and being ignored. Politicians who are trying to convince their citizens that they are serious about the consequences of climate change and its impact, have a new found love with hydrogen hoping to convince their stakeholders that they have found the keys to unlock the most complex problem that the planet has ever confronted. Money, subsidies, and incentives galore are being liberally distributed in the hope that academia and industry can tame the beast of hydrogen and bring it to productive use of humankind. Industry is busy dusting off age old technologies lying on the shelf, once thought of as difficult to bring to market, and see if businesses can be revived. Examples are, alkaline water electrolysers, PEM fuel cells, catalytic cracking of ammonia, etc

These are indeed heady days for the first alphabet of our periodic table which so far attracted the attention only of quantum chemists who took pride in precisely computing it electronic structure and properties based on fundamental quantum mechanics, the only molecule they could do so. As students learning chemistry, hydrogen to us, was the poster boy of the power of quantum mechanics. For me as a chemist, it is also a wonder, that the only element which is not found free on this earth is being touted as the saviour of the planet!

So, what is the truth? Is hydrogen a hype or hope? Are we at the inflection point of a hockey stick curve, where hydrogen is literally and metaphorically ready to take of? How soon can we see the impact of an “hydrogen economy”? As scientists and engineers what should be our messaging to our stakeholders in the government and fellow citizens? How realistic are we in assessing the challenges posed by hydrogen and forecasting its potentials? The first lessons in sustainability science I teach students is to how to assess “unintended consequences” and how to apply “cautionary principles”? How much of these lessons are we bringing to bear on hydrogen-based climate solutions?

Today, I will address some of these concerns with a set of ten paradigms that we need to focus on while searching for an answer. These are as follows:

1. Energy transitions are painfully slow. It took sixty years for coal to gain a share of 50% of the global energy basket. In a similar period, oil gained a share of 40% and natural gas only a share of 25 % of the global energy basket. Successive energy transition is clearly becoming longer. Learn to be patient. We need to make this transition orderly, through incremental steps, each improving on the previous solution. Disruptive transitions will lead to chaos. Define a strategy for energy transition, which is country specific. Tackling climate change and global warming is, both, a moral and economic imperative. We cannot ignore the costs of energy transition or the refusal to recognize trade-offs.

2. Remember the laws of thermodynamics when seeking solutions. All forms of energy are interconvertible. However, every conversion is accompanied by loss of efficiency and increase in entropy. Example, if electricity can be directly used in an application, does it make sense to convert electricity to hydrogen and use it in the same application? Just imagine the overall efficiency of a process where we use solar or wind energy to produce electricity, use electricity to make hydrogen, convert hydrogen to ammonia for easy transportation, convert ammonia back to hydrogen and use the hydrogen as a fuel! If hydrogen can be directly burnt in an ICE, does it make sense to convert the hydrogen to electricity to drive the power train? While cracking natural gas for hydrogen, there is ~ 40 % loss of energy efficiency. When we use electricity from the grid to make hydrogen by electrolysis of water, the loss of energy efficiency is about 78 %. Today when we convert fossil fuels to silicon PV and use it to generate electricity, we do so at an overall energy efficiency of just 3 %. Understand where energy conversion makes sense and where it does not and apply the golden rule, keep the conversion to the minimum and maximize round-trip energy efficiency. We must remember that economic principles and laws of thermodynamics do not work in the same direction. What is economically viable may not necessarily be thermodynamically sustainable.

3. There is a tendency to explore energy intensive conversions merely on the pretext that unlimited low-cost renewable power will be available in the future that will mitigate the economics of high energy chemical and physical transformations. This is a fallacy. Irrespective of its abundance and possible cost, the world cannot move the entire energy demand of powering this planet of close to 20 TW today to renewable energy. This is not because there is not enough wind or sunlight. There are other barriers that will limit its supply. So, it is always a good idea to look for low-energy transformations. Conserving energy is, in the end, always the best solution. All human and economic activities are essentially dissipative of both materials and energy. Every day, we are converting low entropy energy into high-entropy energy. From a sustainability point of view, processes that increase entropy are less sustainable.

4. Keep all technology options open; We should not converge on a solution too quickly; In the new carbon-constrained world we all aspire to live in many technologies will be relevant and will co-exist, based on demographic, geographic, climactic, and economic criteria. We are unlikely to have a “one shoe fit all” solutions like we often had in the post-industrial fossil-fuel driven world. Unlike in the carbon-intensive world where economy of scale was important, economy of scope will be more important in a carbonconstrained world. Rapid development of technologies is occurring in energy related areas, that may make any given option irrelevant anytime. Example, if the battery recharging time for an EV is reduced to a few minutes, will the advantage of hydrogen filling time for a fuel cell vehicle be relevant?

5. Let us understand the unintended consequences of a hydrogen economy. Hydrogen production, use, storage, and transportation are all full of safety and environmental challenges. Unlike bound hydrogen which is safe, anthropogenic “free” hydrogen is reactive, a strong reducing agent and flammable over a wide explosive range. A hydrogen flame is invisible. Global fugitive leaks of hydrogen across its full value chain must be kept below 1% to preserve its climate benefits. At a 10 % leakage, the decarbonizing power of hydrogen will be lost since the GWP of hydrogen is six times more potent than carbon dioxide!! The world has had experience of over fifty years in handling natural gas; yet we have inadequate data on a global scale on methane leaks. The U.S. Environmental Protection Agency estimates that about 6.5 million metric tons of methane leak from the oil and gas supply chain each year, around 1% of total natural gas production. Many believe it is much larger on a global scale. Natural gas is about 8.5 times as dense as hydrogen, and dense gases are easier (more energy efficient) to move than less dense ones. Same is true for ammonia leaks. If the entire shipping industry switches to ammonia as a fuel, it will consume 700 million tons of ammonia, more than four times the current production of ammonia. Ammonia leaks leads to nitrous oxide which is a potent GHG.

6. Define a “Green Taxonomy” and “Transition Framework” which is science based, practical to implement and consistent with India’s nationally defined climate goals and in sync with global practices. They should cover three dimensions, local, regional, and global. Make it robust enough to prevent easy gaming and greenwashing. Catchy colour coded adjectives must be understood uniformly to ensure cross border trade between nations. Do we have a common understanding of “green coal,” “black aluminium” or for that matter, “green hydrogen”? Transition taxonomy must not only involve activities that decrease emissions but also must look at how entities are going through the transition. It must map both the journey and the destination and must be consistent with India’s own economic development agenda.

7. Government and public policies on energy transition must be goal driven and technology agnostic. There are risks in governments choosing winners or subsidising specific technologies. Much of the subsidies that government provides to technology is clouded by the power of the interested lobby. In the next few decades as world goes through energy transition, there will be few winners and many losers. The energy transition landscape is likely to be littered with technology graveyards.

8. Public -private partnership is essential for deploying green technologies at scale. Public R&D is not mere blue-sky, but it must shape markets and drive innovation where private sector is risk averse. Government needs to fund, both, radical new technologies and targeted development with commercial potential. Symbiotic, not parasitic relationship between sate funded R&D and private sector must have three dimensions: set priorities for private sector R&D, drive greater collaboration between early-stage research and privately funded translation and incentivize private sector to bring new technologies to markets.

9. Energy transitions are not painless. Recognize the “impossible trinity of net-zero.” Plan and prepare for political and social disruption and upheavals. The nature of jobs in oldenergy economy may not be the same in the new-energy economy. Greater focus on education, upskilling and reskilling is necessary.

10.Communicate responsibly in a manner that public trust in science and technology is enhanced and not eroded. Exaggerated claims or hype do not serve the cause of science. As scientists we get caught up in proving something is possible, but we forget to ask whether it will be practical. When we report scientific advances the society at large does not have the tools to tell whether it will bring practical benefits. Clearly for a technology to be practically implemented, it must be possible. But only a small subset of technologies that are possible will become practical.

Friends, let me conclude my observations with a few philosophical thoughts. Given the commitment to reach net-zero and the diminishing window of time that is available, we must take decisions sooner rather than later. However, in our rush to take decisions, let us not commit ourself to another road from which there will be no return. We should also not escape our responsibilities by pretending that all problems can be solved better and at a lower cost through the mirage of research. Terms like “green energy” and “hydrogen economy” have an important psychological impact on our society and, as scientists and engineers, society looks to us as harbingers of hope in a world where we are bombarded by grim headlines every day. We have great responsibility in speaking the truth and educating the public and politicians about the real options. In closing, I am reminded of an old cartoon in the Pogo series that said, “We have met the real enemy and he is us.” Let this not become true.
